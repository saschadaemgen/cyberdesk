    Everything you browse lives only in memory and is gone when CyberDesk closes. As a standing safeguard, each launch also wipes any browsing cache or content that ever reached the disk - so nothing you browsed survives on disk, even if an update ever wrote some by accident. It only ever touches the browsing profile: your Tor state (so its protection carries across restarts) and your saved session are never touched.

    What this does
    What it does
    Solves every measurable fingerprint vector: stable signals are clamped to common values (your local fonts are hidden behind a standard set, GPU strings and screen and window size read as an ordinary machine, codecs and math are normalized), and measured signals carry fresh per-session noise (canvas, WebGL, audio, text metrics, high-resolution timers). The noise is stable within a session but changes on the next launch or a new identity, so a site cannot link one session to the next.
    What stays real
    Your operating system, browser identity (User-Agent), and language are left untouched and mutually consistent - no half-spoofing that would make you more identifiable.

    Tracking resistance makes you hard to profile and link across sites and sessions: stable signals are clamped to common values and measured signals carry fresh per-session noise, so the fingerprint a tracker records today doesn't match the one it sees tomorrow. For IP anonymity, add CyberDesk's built-in per-window Tor (above) - the two layers are designed to work together.

    What this does

    A new identity re-seeds the noise your browser adds to every fingerprint surface, so a fresh set of sessions can't be linked to the old one. It works strongest between pages and sessions: the per-window New identity now button (in each window's tracking-resistance menu) and on restart reload with a fresh identity immediately, while automatic rotation re-seeds the basis for what you open next and drives the countdown on the grid.
